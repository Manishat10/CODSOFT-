  • EARLY LIFE

     Vivekananda was born as Narendranath Datta (name shortened to Narendra or Naren) in a Bengali family in his ancestral home at 3 Gourmohan Mukherjee Street in Calcutta, the capital of British India, on 12 January 1863 during the Makar Sankranti festival. He belonged to a traditional family and was one of nine siblings. His father, Vishwanath Datta, was an attorney at the Calcutta High Court. Durgacharan Datta, Narendra's grandfather was a Sanskrit and Persian scholar who left his family and became a monk at age twenty-five. His mother, Bhubaneswari Devi, was a devout housewife. The progressive, rational attitude of Narendra's father and the religious temperament of his mother helped shape his thinking and personality.Narendranath was interested in spirituality from a young age and used to meditate before the images of deities such as Shiva, Rama, Sita, and Mahavir Hanuman. He was fascinated by wandering ascetics and monks. Narendra was mischievous and restless as a child, and his parents often had difficulty controlling him. His mother said, "I prayed to Shiva for a son and he has sent me one of his demons".

  • EDUCATION

     During his formative years, Narendranath (Vivekananda) exhibited exceptional intellect and diverse interests. Enrolled at Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar's Metropolitan Institution at the age of eight, he later attended the General Assembly's Institution (now known as the Scottish Church College), where he delved into a wide array of subjects, including philosophy, religion, history, social science, art, and literature. His academic prowess was evident when he scored the sole first-division marks in the Presidency College entrance exam upon his family's return to Calcutta in 1879. Narendranath was a voracious reader, deeply engaging with Hindu scriptures like the Vedas, Upanishads, and Bhagavad Gita, as well as Western philosophers such as Hume, Kant, Hegel, and Darwin. Narendranath's memory and speed reading abilities were extraordinary. He could quote pages from books like Pickwick Papers verbatim and recall historical details in debates, surprising even experts. At times, he left professors astounded by reciting and interpreting complex verses from texts he had just read. His prodigious memory led some to describe him as a "shrutidhara," a person with remarkable memorization skills. In 1884, he completed his Bachelor of Arts degree and drew praise from figures like William Hastie, the principal of Christian College, Calcutta, who predicted Narendranath's remarkable future achievements. His academic pursuits, wide-ranging interests, and extraordinary memory paved the way for his profound impact on philosophy and spirituality.

  • CELEBRATIONS

     Swami Vivekananda's legacy is remembered and celebrated through various avenues. His birthday, the 12th of January, is observed as National Youth Day in India, focusing on empowering the younger generation with his ideals. Additionally, the 11th of September, the date of his iconic speech at the Parliament of Religions, is recognized as "World Brotherhood Day," emphasizing the universal values of unity and harmony. The global celebrations of his 150th birth anniversary in 2013 further highlight his enduring impact on individuals and communities, both within India and around the world.
